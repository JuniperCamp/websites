Naturism is a way of life in harmony with nature, the practice of communal nudity, and the encouragement of self-respect, respect for others and for the environment.

However, there are many different naturists in the world that live by their own definition.

Below are a few types of naturism. Many types intersect with one another. For example, ethical naturism typically coincides with family naturism.

  • Activist Naturism utilizes the human body—not necessarily for naturism's natural qualities— to call attention to social ills and problems.
  • Casual Naturism is nudity without philosophy. Practicioners tend to like being naked without a deeper reason.
  • Ethical Naturism is the belief that accepting the human body by rejecting social stigmas and minimizing your impact on nature are moral, ethical, and spiritual imperatives. It is a philosophy that encompasses the entirety of a practioner's daily life—with or without clothes.
  • Family Naturism incorporates children and teenagers in an attempt to encourage body confidence and care for nature at an early age.
  • Home Naturism focuses on being naked and natural within the confines of a home whether alone or not.
  • Outdoor Naturism explores the world naked whether permissible or not, such as skinny dipping or hiking.
  • Religious Naturism weaves nudity and nature together within an existing religious context, such as the Digambara monks in Jainism.
  • Sex-positive Naturism accepts that humans are sexual by nature and that topics of sex be discussed without taboo.
  • Sexual Naturism treats nudity and naturalness as sexually explicit and for sexual satisfaction.
  • Social Naturism is when two or more individuals participate in an activity naked.
